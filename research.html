"The Macroeconomic Effect of Fiscal Announcements: News-Based Evidence from European Countries"

Abstract: Fiscal policy is usually announced before it is implemented, and such announcements can affect macroeconomic conditions regardless of whether the announced measures are actually implemented. In this paper, we construct two new quarterly fiscal announcement indices—one specific to public spending and the other to taxation—based on press reports from four major euro area economies: Spain, France, Italy, and Germany (1999–2025). Using large language models, whose classifications we validate manually, we systematically code each news item according to the fiscal stance it conveys, the responsible institutional level and the type of communication. We then incorporate these specific expenditure and taxation indices into a battery of bayesian vector autoregressive models, estimated both by country and in a grouped panel framework, with recursive identification. We demonstrate that, consistently across the four countries—although the responses for Germany are somewhat less stable—signals of increased public spending anticipate actual increases in government expenditure and generate clear and persistent expansions in GDP and consumption, while announcements of tax increases boost tax revenues but produce more moderate and shorter-lived contractions. Overall, the results confirm that press reports contain useful information for identifying the shocks caused by fiscal announcements.
